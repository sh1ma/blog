I signed up for a driving school camp to get a regular Class 1 driver's license. I went with [Sohgoh Driving School](https://www.sohgoh.co.jp/).

This was actually my third attempt at getting a license, and my second time doing a camp.

I think I first tried to get a license when I was 19 or 20.  
I wanted to knock it out early, so I went with a camp and traveled all the way to Yamagata — only to head home on day two because of homesickness.  
Well, strictly speaking, it wasn't homesickness.
I wasn't in a great mental state to begin with, and when I arrived at the inn — out in the middle of nowhere with not a single shop nearby — it hit me: "I can't survive out here on my own." My mental state fell apart and I dropped out.
They told me to pay the learner's permit exam fee in cash, but there was nowhere near the school or the inn to withdraw money. I felt completely stuck.

For the second attempt, I took the previous failure into account and tried a local commuter school. And I went for MT, no less. I figured, "If I can just commute, this'll be a breeze" — and I was completely wrong.  
But I was close, you know? I was so close.

I had already gotten my learner's permit and finished all the Stage 2 classroom lessons. But I was juggling work at the same time, and my mental state wasn't good. Sorry for existing.
I dropped out with just three practical lessons and the final exam left. Why would you drop out there? And on MT, no less.

Still, those two failures taught me the following:

- _Don't try to get a license when you're in a bad mental state (because when you're in a bad mental state, you can't take lessons)_
- _Don't try to get a license while working (because you'll burn out and your mental health will crash)_
- _Driving school camps are wao! And go somewhere with a convenience store (because if you end up in the sticks, your spirit breaks and your mental health crashes)_

Apparently a mental weakling like me needs a bit of strategy to get a license.

Honestly, commuting no longer felt realistic. Getting to the school takes energy on its own, but the bigger issue is time.
I work (basically) full-time, so carving out the hours is hard.
Which left me with only one option: a camp.

So this is my report on trying a driving school camp.

## Getting the Learner's Permit

Driving school is split into two parts, called "Stage 1" and "Stage 2." You can't start Stage 2 until you finish Stage 1 and get your learner's permit, so
after enrolling, the first goal is clearing Stage 1 and getting the permit.

### Day One

We met at Hamamatsu Station at 10:40.  
I got there early and had to wait for the other participants to show up, but by that point some of them had already hit it off with each other, which made me panic a bit.

**"I have to figure out how to talk to someone too...!"**

Driven by that panic, I worked up the nerve on the shuttle bus from the station to the school and struck up a conversation with a friendly-looking young guy.
Turned out he was 18 — and a high schooler. Right. Of course most people doing camps at this time of year are students. Realizing that, my behavior suddenly felt embarrassingly out of place for my age.

By the way, after we got to the school and finished the aptitude test, I noticed the guy from the bus was gone. Apparently he'd failed the aptitude test and been sent home. I didn't even know you could fail that.

After that, they explained the accommodation and the rules for our stay, we had our first practical lesson, and then we headed back to the hotel.

### Day Two Through the Learner's Permit Exam

I couldn't make any friends, so I ended up spending my time alone.  
I figured I could at least use the downtime to get some work done, but I got zero traction and gave up partway through.  
I was lonely, so every night I hopped into voice chat on a private Discord server with friends — that was my only conversation outside the day's lessons. Big thanks to the friends who kept me company.

In the practical lessons, from day one I was getting comments like "You're good at this" — which, well, of course. This was my second time trying to get a license. I made it to exam day without any real trouble.

Classroom lessons were done online: I watched video lectures on my own device from my hotel room.  
Since I just had to finish all 10 videos before the learner's permit exam, I thought, "Sweet, I can just leave them playing." But you had to keep the camera on so they could check you were paying attention, and the system would forcibly end the lesson if it thought you weren't — so I ended up actually listening.  
That said, paying attention makes the later written exams easier, so it worked out.

Before the learner's permit exam, I also had to take something called an "effectiveness measurement." It's 50 true/false questions worth two points each, with a passing line of 90 — so 45 correct answers to pass. You had to clear it before exam day. If you couldn't, you were locked in for an extended stay.
I was surprised they slotted it into my schedule right on day two, but I somehow passed on the first try. Got a 92.

### Learner's Permit Exam Day

The learner's permit exam is split into a "completion test" and a "learner's permit written exam." Only people who pass the completion test can take the written exam, and passing that gets you the permit.

That day, while waiting for the completion test, two people from my cohort (?) came up and talked to me, and when I responded with a goofy grin, we hit it off. We ate together and chatted that day.
I ended up hanging out with those two through the last day of the camp.

I was pretty nervous on exam day, but talking with people took the edge off, and I passed without any issues.

## Getting to the Final Exam

Right after getting the learner's permit, you move on to Stage 2. In fact, I was pushed straight into a Stage 2 on-road lesson the same day I passed the written exam.

Honestly, aside from being out on actual roads, Stage 2 isn't really different from Stage 1, so there isn't much to write about.

But unlike during Stage 1, I could grab lunch and chat with friends between lessons, which was fun.

### The Final Exam

The final exam is just a practical test. The corresponding written exam is taken at your local driver's license center.

The exam is "on-road driving + (three-point turn | parallel parking)"; whether you get the three-point turn or parallel parking depends on the test route. (I think there were about six or seven routes.)
I got a relatively easy route, but I slammed the brakes hard early on and honestly thought I might have failed — turns out I passed. Nice. I did get chewed out afterward, though.

## Everything Else

### Accommodation

I stayed at [Kuretake Inn Central Hamamatsu](https://kuretake-inn.com/hotel/hamamatsu-central).

Half the room was taken up by the bed, and half of what was left was taken up by the desk.  
It felt cramped, but as long as I didn't spread my stuff out too much, there was enough space to live, so I figured it was fine.

### Meals

Breakfast was buffet style. I ate a single plate of fruit punch every day.  
Lunch was at the driving school cafeteria. It wasn't amazing, but it was decent enough to eat.

Dinner was the tricky one.  
For dinner, the hotel gave us "meal coupons" — if you used them at designated restaurants, you got 1000 yen off.  
At first I thought, "Free dinner out — amazing!" But there weren't nearly as many options as I expected. (More accurately, the designated restaurants were kind of far.)
Before the learner's permit exam, I was eating pilaf pretty much every night at a sketchy-looking (?) Asian restaurant across from the hotel called [Sarasaya](https://maps.app.goo.gl/uUmUxG5jP9ox6zBSA).
It was fairly spicy, but after the learner's permit exam I could go eat with friends, so I honestly started looking forward to it every day.

I forget which day it was, but I also went to [Charcoal-Grilled Restaurant Sawayaka Hamamatsu Entetsu](https://g.co/kgs/HZdQeTc). I grabbed a numbered ticket around 11 and then waited about two hours (!) before getting in.  
As for the taste — honestly, it was more of a "I waited two hours for this...?" kind of moment. But hey, it was more of a bucket-list thing.

### Miscellaneous

- I ended up buying nail clippers and an ear pick partway through. If you're worried about it, might be worth packing them.
- The beds are insanely hard. It's basically like sleeping on concrete, so brace yourself.
- If you get back to the hotel too early, they won't let you into your room because they're "still cleaning" or whatever. I got caught out twice. On the days lessons ended at 11, I couldn't get in until 15:00, which was rough.

## Wrap-up

- I went to a driving school camp
- Even a social wreck like me managed to graduate
- I even ended up making a few friends

That's all.
