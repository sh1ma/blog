title: The Time I Went to Nagoya
publishedAt: "2024-08-31"
---

I've actually got plenty of things I could write about on the blog, it's just the time and energy... which is to say, motivation. I don't have any.  
Sitting down to write a proper article on a specific topic feels impossible right now, so I'm going to bang out a quick recap of my trip to Nagoya instead. I'm writing this while waiting for a friend at a cafe, so I'll wrap up whenever they show up.

The biggest thing this month was, hands down, going to Nagoya. I'd been wanting to try a solo long-distance road trip for a while, and since a friend lives there and it was at a convenient distance, I went.  
On the day I left, I hung out with friends, got home at midnight, napped until 2 a.m., and then hit the road. For some reason I don't remember feeling tired at all. I must have been hyped.  
On the way, I passed through Gotemba. Honestly, I'd always thought that even getting to Gotemba counted as "long distance," so realizing I was still only in the early stages of the drive was demoralizing.  
At Gotemba JCT I merged onto the Shin-Tomei Expressway. Right after getting on I was having a blast — you can do up to 120 km/h and the road is wide — but after about 30 minutes I got bored and drowsy. I'd always thought "there's no way you'd get sleepy while driving, let alone at 120 km/h," but turns out it really does happen.  
The drowsiness scared me enough that I took several breaks along the way. My first stop was around 3 a.m. at Suruga Bay Numazu SA, then Shizuoka SA and Hamamatsu SA after that. "Break" just meant using the restroom, going back to the car, reclining the seat, and shutting my eyes for about 10 minutes — but even that made a real difference compared to not stopping at all. Driving nonstop really does drain your focus.  
As I drove, I could feel the sky changing color. By the last stretch, the sun was fully up, and I realized I was carving through big mountains via tunnel after tunnel.

I got to Nagoya around 6:30 a.m. I'd left in the middle of the night specifically to avoid any traffic at all, but standing alone in an unfamiliar city at 6:30, with a little loneliness, nothing to do, and the fatigue of an all-nighter — it put me in a pretty gloomy mood.

I spent an hour, maybe closer to two, reclined in the driver's seat with my eyes closed in a Lawson parking lot. Sorry for hogging the space!

After that, I killed time a bunch of different ways and checked into the Tokyu Inn right at 3 p.m. I was completely spent — mentally and physically — so stepping into the hotel room felt like being saved.

I was tempted to fall asleep the second I got in, but I was also hungry. While I was debating what to do, my friend messaged me something like "can you meet up at 6?" and I said "yeah I can." (Could I, really?)

There was still time until 6, and my friend was going to eat somewhere else, so I decided to feed myself first. I headed to a place called "Yabaton," which several friends had recommended to me. When a friend had once shown me a photo of a Yabaton miso katsu set meal or something, I'd blurted out "this for just under 2000 yen?" — but once I actually ate it, it was seriously good. I want to have it again. I think I ordered the larger set. I also think I went through about a quarter of the mustard on the table. Sorry about that.

Stomach full, drowsiness came back for round two, but I somehow made it to meet my friend. When they asked "where do you want to go?" I answered "Nagoya Castle" without hesitation. Nagoya Castle — one of Japan's three great castles. I'm reasonably into Japanese history (even though I didn't actually know that much about Nagoya Castle specifically), so I really wanted to see it. It was a bit far, so my friend was reluctant, but they took me in the end. It was about 10 minutes by bus from Sakae, I think.

I remember we had to walk a while after getting off the bus before the keep came into view. It must have been around 7 p.m. by then. There were a lot of people in yukata around us, so I wondered if there was a festival going on somewhere — but as we got closer to the castle it became clear they were all heading into the castle grounds. There was a festival being held right inside Nagoya Castle.  
So I ended up crashing a summer festival by accident. I hadn't been to any kind of festival since sixth grade, so watching people do Bon dances with the food stalls lined up gave me this nostalgic feeling. The last summer festival I'd been to was loud, muggy, and packed with people — I hate crowds — and all I remembered was wanting to go home. But this time, I could just enjoy it. My friend and I each got a grape candy from a stall. (The vendor threw in an extra one on the house because we "looked close." Thank you!) That was the first time I learned that you can get grape candy, not just apple candy.

And so, after enjoying summer — a season I've hated — for the first time in over a decade, day one wrapped up.

My friend is about to show up, and this feels like a decent stopping point, so I'll leave it here. Day two was also great — we went to see a waterfall, sat by Lake Biwa at sunset — and I'd love to write about it, but honestly, whether I'll ever feel like writing another Nagoya post is doubtful. So this might be it for the Nagoya trip.
